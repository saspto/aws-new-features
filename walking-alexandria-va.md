# Walking Alexandria, Virginia
### A Complete Guide to Exploring America's Most Walkable Historic City

*By Workshop Participant*

---

## A Note to the Reader

Alexandria, Virginia is one of America's most rewarding cities to explore on foot. Tucked along the western bank of the Potomac River just south of the nation's capital, this compact city packs centuries of American history, vibrant neighborhoods, independent shops, acclaimed restaurants, and miles of waterfront paths into an area small enough to cross in an afternoon.

This book is an invitation. Whether you are a first-time visitor arriving by Metro from Washington, D.C., a longtime resident who has never ventured beyond King Street, or a dedicated urban hiker looking for a new city to map with your feet, the chapters ahead will guide you down cobblestone alleys, through quiet residential squares, along the shimmering riverfront, and out into neighborhoods that most tourists never find.

Each walk in this guide has been chosen to reveal a different facet of Alexandria: its colonial origins, its Civil War scars, its African American heritage, its Victorian residential streets, its modern waterfront renaissance, and its surprising natural edges. Together they form a portrait of a city that rewards curiosity.

Lace up your most comfortable shoes. Let's walk.

## Chapter 1: Alexandria at a Glance

### The Lay of the Land

Alexandria sits on roughly 15 square miles of Northern Virginia, bordered by the Potomac River to the east, Fairfax County to the west and south, and Arlington County to the north. Within that space you will find three distinct areas relevant to walkers:

**Old Town Alexandria** — The historic core, occupying the northeastern corner of the city. Old Town's grid of streets dates to the 1740s and contains the greatest concentration of 18th- and 19th-century architecture in the region. This is where most visitors begin, and many never leave.

**Del Ray** — A residential neighborhood northwest of Old Town centered on Mount Vernon Avenue. Del Ray has the feel of a 1950s Main Street that survived intact, with local restaurants, bookshops, and a strong community identity. It is one of Alexandria's best-kept secrets.

**Arlandria / Chirilagua** — Where Alexandria meets Arlington to the north, this vibrant working-class neighborhood is home to one of the largest Salvadoran communities on the East Coast. Walking here means encountering pupuserías, murals, and a deeply human energy absent from the more polished parts of town.

**West Alexandria / Seminary Hill** — The quieter, hillier western portions of the city, where you will find the Virginia Theological Seminary, Landmark Mall's redevelopment corridor, and largely residential streets.

**The Waterfront** — Running along the Potomac from Jones Point in the south to the northern city limit, this corridor of parks, marinas, and promenades is the city's outdoor living room.

### A Brief History in Walking Distance

Alexandria was established by Virginia colonial legislature in 1749, surveyed in part by a teenage George Washington, and quickly became one of the busiest tobacco ports in North America. Its streets were trodden by Washington, Jefferson, and Lafayette. Robert E. Lee grew up here; the boyhood home where he lived from age five still stands on Oronoco Street.

During the Civil War, Union forces occupied Alexandria almost immediately after Virginia's secession, turning it into a massive logistics and hospital city. The war left visible marks: the bullet hole in the Marshall House hotel (site of the war's first officer casualty), the Contraband and Freedmen's Cemetery where formerly enslaved people buried their dead, and the network of forts whose earthworks still rise from the hills.

After the war, Alexandria grew steadily through the Victorian era, the streetcar suburbs of the early 20th century, and the federal expansion of World War II. Urban renewal in the 1960s and 1970s threatened to demolish the old waterfront warehouses; a preservation movement saved them, transforming what is now the heart of the tourist economy.

Today the city is both a heritage destination and a living, breathing community of roughly 165,000 people. The tension between history and growth, between preservation and development, plays out visibly on every walk.

## Chapter 2: Before You Go

### Practical Essentials

**Footwear** — Old Town's brick sidewalks are beautiful and uneven. Comfortable, broken-in shoes with solid soles will serve you far better than sandals or dress shoes. For the waterfront trails, any athletic shoe is fine.

**Weather** — Alexandria experiences all four seasons with conviction. Spring (March–May) and fall (September–November) are ideal: mild temperatures, low humidity, and the city's trees at their spectacular best. Summer (June–August) is hot and humid; plan waterfront walks for early morning. Winter walks have their own rewards — fewer crowds, bare trees revealing hidden architecture, and the occasional snowfall that transforms the brick streets into something out of a Dickens novel.

**Distance** — All walks in this guide are between 1.5 and 5 miles. The Old Town core walk is about 2.5 miles; the waterfront walk is 4 miles round-trip; the neighborhood walks average 3 miles.

**Getting Here** — The King Street–Old Town Metro station (Blue and Yellow lines) deposits you four blocks from the Potomac River at the foot of King Street. From there, the entire Old Town grid is walkable. A free King Street Trolley runs along King Street between the Metro and the waterfront from early morning to late night.

**Parking** — If you drive, the city operates several garages and surface lots. The Potomac Yard parking area offers easy access to the northern neighborhoods.

**Maps** — The Alexandria Visitors Center at 221 King Street offers free paper maps and knowledgeable staff. The City of Alexandria also maintains an excellent online interactive map of walking trails.

**Water and Food** — Alexandria is extraordinarily well supplied with restaurants, cafés, and water fountains along all major routes. You will not go hungry or thirsty.

## Chapter 3: Walk One — The Old Town Historic Core

**Distance:** 2.5 miles  
**Estimated Time:** 2–3 hours (without extended stops)  
**Terrain:** Brick and paved sidewalks, gentle inclines  
**Best Time:** Any season; weekday mornings are least crowded

### Starting Point: Market Square

Begin at Market Square, at the intersection of King Street and North Royal Street. This is the oldest continuously operating farmers market in the United States, held every Saturday morning since 1753. The square is anchored by Alexandria's City Hall, whose current building dates to 1871 but whose site has served as the civic center since the city's founding.

Stand in the square and orient yourself. King Street runs due east to the Potomac River, four blocks away. It runs west toward the Metro station and beyond. North-south streets cross it in alphabetical order: Fairfax, Royal, Pitt, Saint Asaph, Washington, Columbus, Alfred, Patrick, Henry. This orderly grid was Alexandria's great gift to its walkers.

### King Street East: The Commercial Heart

Walk east on King Street toward the river. This is Alexandria's main tourist corridor, lined with independent boutiques, art galleries, wine bars, and restaurants. Note that despite the commercial character, nearly every building you pass is a genuine 18th- or 19th-century structure.

At 121 King Street, look for **Gadsby's Tavern** on your left as you reach North Royal Street (actually, Gadsby's is at 134 N. Royal Street — step one block north). One of the finest surviving examples of Georgian public architecture in America, Gadsby's operated as a tavern and hotel from the 1770s through the early 19th century. George Washington held his final review of troops here. Today the building operates as a museum and restaurant; if hours permit, step inside to see the original ballroom and taproom.

Continue east on King Street. At the corner of King and Pitt Streets, look up at the **Stabler-Leadbeater Apothecary Museum** on your right (105½ South Fairfax Street, just off King). Founded in 1792, this pharmacy served Alexandria's residents until 1933 and was the site where Robert E. Lee received his orders to suppress John Brown's raid at Harpers Ferry. The interior has been preserved essentially unchanged; its collection of hand-blown glass apothecary bottles is extraordinary.

### The Riverfront

King Street ends at the **Alexandria City Waterfront**, a public park stretching along the Potomac. The marina here hosts a mix of pleasure boats and the occasional tall ship. Sit on the steps and watch the river, which is perhaps a mile wide at this point, with the wooded shores of National Harbor, Maryland visible on the far bank.

The waterfront park replaced the old torpedo factory and warehouses that once dominated this stretch. The **Torpedo Factory Art Center**, immediately to your north, occupies what was literally a World War I and II torpedo manufacturing facility. Today it houses over 80 working artist studios — printmakers, jewelers, painters, sculptors, ceramicists — all open to the public at no charge. This is one of the finest art center facilities on the East Coast and is completely free to enter.

Spend time wandering the Torpedo Factory's three floors, watching artists at work and browsing their galleries. Then step back outside and walk north along the waterfront to see the **Alexandria Archaeology Museum**, which occupies the third floor of the Torpedo Factory building and houses artifacts excavated from Alexandria's soil over decades.

### North Royal Street and Oronoco Street

From the waterfront, turn west (away from the river) on Prince Street, walking one block, then turn north on North Royal Street. This residential stretch of Royal Street is one of Old Town's most beautiful, lined with Federal-style townhouses from the early 19th century, their brick facades painted in faded ochres, blues, and creams.

Walk north on Royal Street to **Oronoco Street** and turn left (west). At 607 Oronoco Street stands the **Lee-Fendall House**, built in 1785 and home to 37 members of the Lee family over the course of 118 years. One block west at 611 Cameron Street (the corner of Cameron and Washington) is the **George Washington Masonic National Memorial**, which dominates the city's western skyline — though we will visit it properly in a later walk.

At 301 North Washington Street, at the corner of Oronoco, is the **Robert E. Lee Boyhood Home**, a Federal townhouse where Lee lived from ages 5 to 18. The house is privately owned and not open for tours, but its exterior and the surrounding streets give you a sense of Lee's Alexandria childhood.

### Captain's Row and Gentry Row

Return east to the waterfront via Prince Street. The 100 block of Prince Street between Fairfax and Lee Streets is called **Captain's Row**, its original cobblestones intact. According to local tradition, this block was paved by Hessian prisoners of war after the Revolution. The cobblestones are original, genuinely rough underfoot, and utterly evocative. Walk this block slowly.

One block north, the 100 block of King Street between Fairfax and Lee is called **Gentry Row** — a line of late 18th-century merchant's houses whose facades remain almost entirely unchanged. Stand mid-block and squint: the 21st century largely disappears.

### Return to Market Square

Walk west on King Street back to Market Square, your starting point. You have just covered the essential skeleton of Old Town's historic core. Everything you saw was built before 1860; much of it before 1800.

## Chapter 4: Walk Two — The Waterfront Trail

**Distance:** 4 miles (round trip, south to north)  
**Estimated Time:** 2–3 hours  
**Terrain:** Paved trail, flat  
**Best Time:** Early morning or late afternoon; spectacular at sunset

### The Potomac Heritage Trail

Alexandria's waterfront trail is part of the larger Potomac Heritage National Scenic Trail, a network of routes that follows the Potomac River from its headwaters in West Virginia to the Chesapeake Bay. In Alexandria, a continuous trail runs along the riverfront from **Jones Point Park** in the south to the city's northern boundary — roughly two miles each way.

### Jones Point: Where Alexandria Began

Begin at **Jones Point Park**, at the southernmost tip of Alexandria where the Potomac and Hunting Creek meet. This is one of the most historically significant and least-visited spots in the city.

Under the **Jones Point Lighthouse**, built in 1855 and the oldest lighthouse in the Potomac, a granite marker embedded in the ground marks one corner of the original survey of the District of Columbia. When Pierre Charles L'Enfant was surveying the new federal district in 1791, he placed this stone as the southernmost boundary marker. The marker still sits in its original location, now inside a small protective enclosure. You are standing at the geometric corner of a 10-mile diamond surveyed 230 years ago.

Jones Point Park is also the site of a significant piece of Civil War history: Union forces built extensive earthwork fortifications here in 1861. Walking the park's perimeter, you can still make out the gentle undulations of those earthworks beneath the grass.

The park offers excellent views south down the Potomac toward Woodbridge and, on clear days, far beyond. A colony of osprey nests in the trees along the water's edge; bring binoculars if you have them.

### Walking North Along the Shore

From Jones Point, the paved trail follows the waterfront north, passing through a series of parks and preserved green spaces that form the city's eastern edge. This stretch is popular with cyclists and joggers in the morning; by midday it tends toward a pleasant mix of families, dog walkers, and tourists.

You will pass the **Waterfront Park** adjacent to King Street, where you may have ended Walk One. Continue north, passing the historic **Robinson Terminal** warehouse, now converted to restaurants and commercial space, and the **Founders Park**, a long green space with benches facing the river.

### Oronoco Bay Park

At the foot of Oronoco Street, **Oronoco Bay Park** offers a quiet sitting area and some of the best mid-river views in Old Town. Across the river, you can see the Maryland shoreline and, on clear days, the towers of National Harbor to the southeast.

This park is also the departure point for **water taxi service** to the National Mall in Washington, D.C. If you are visiting Alexandria as part of a larger D.C. trip, the water taxi is a genuinely delightful alternative to the Metro.

### Montgomery Park and the Northern Shore

Continue north past the park and through the transitions zone where Old Town's tourist-oriented waterfront gives way to the quieter residential and light-industrial northern edge of the city. The architecture changes: warehouses from the mid-20th century, a working marina, older light-industrial buildings in various states of conversion to residential and commercial use.

This northern stretch is less curated than the Old Town core — and more interesting for it. You see Alexandria as a functioning city, not just a heritage attraction.

Turn around at the city limit and return south, enjoying the now-familiar views from the opposite direction. Morning and afternoon light transform the Potomac entirely.

## Chapter 5: Walk Three — Del Ray and the Avenue

**Distance:** 3 miles  
**Estimated Time:** 2 hours  
**Terrain:** Flat paved sidewalks  
**Best Time:** Saturday morning (for the farmers market) or any weekend afternoon

### A Different Alexandria

Del Ray feels like a different city from Old Town — quieter, more residential, less oriented toward tourism. Where Old Town looks backward to the 18th century, Del Ray is solidly 20th-century American: bungalows and craftsman houses, a classic commercial strip, a strong neighborhood identity forged partly in reaction against Old Town's dominance of Alexandria's public image.

Del Ray developed as a streetcar suburb in the early 1900s, annexed by Alexandria in 1930, and spent the mid-century in the quiet obscurity that preserved its character. Today it is one of the most sought-after residential neighborhoods in Northern Virginia.

### The Avenue

**Mount Vernon Avenue** is the spine of Del Ray, running from Braddock Road in the south to Commonwealth Avenue in the north — a length of about a mile. Walk its full length.

The Avenue's commercial blocks are a lesson in what a functioning local economy looks like. There are no chain restaurants here (with one or two exceptions). Instead you will find: independent bakeries, a used bookstore, yoga studios, art galleries, a cheese shop, a fishmonger, small ethnic restaurants representing El Salvador, Ethiopia, Vietnam, and half a dozen other countries, and the kind of hardware store where the staff actually know where things are.

On the first Saturday of each month (April through December), the Del Ray Farmers Market sets up at the intersection of Mount Vernon and Oxford Avenues. This is a genuinely local market: smaller than the Old Town Saturday market, more neighborhood-focused, and in some ways more interesting.

### Side Streets

Del Ray's residential streets east and west of Mount Vernon Avenue reward exploration. Walk east on **Oxford Avenue** through a block of 1940s bungalows, then south on **Hume Avenue** through the deeper residential core. The front porches are occupied in the evenings; the gardens are elaborate and personal.

At the southern end of Del Ray, **The Birchmere** music venue at 3701 Mount Vernon Avenue has hosted Americana, folk, and roots music since 1966. It is not visually distinguished, but its history is embedded in the fabric of American popular music. Emmylou Harris, Alison Krauss, Mary Chapin Carpenter, and hundreds of others have played this room.

### Braddock Road and the Diagonal

Return to Old Town via **Braddock Road**, which runs diagonally northeast toward the river, cutting across Alexandria's otherwise orderly grid. This diagonal reflects the old road network that predated the city's formal 1749 survey — Braddock Road follows an ancient route that Braddock's army used during the French and Indian War.

Walk Braddock Road northeast toward the river, noting how the architecture transitions from Del Ray's bungalows through older residential blocks to the Federal period townhouses of Old Town's northern edge.

## Chapter 6: Walk Four — African American Heritage

**Distance:** 2.5 miles  
**Estimated Time:** 2–3 hours  
**Terrain:** Paved sidewalks, some uneven brick  
**Best Time:** Any season; combine with a visit to the Freedom House Museum

### A History That Belongs Here

No walk in Alexandria is complete without engaging its African American history — a history that is not separate from the city's mainstream heritage but central to it. Alexandria was a major node in the domestic slave trade, home to one of the largest slave-trading operations in the United States. It was also home to communities of free Black residents before the Civil War, to Contraband camps during the war, and to generations of African American families who built durable institutions under Jim Crow and contributed to the civil rights movement.

These stories are told in the landscape if you know where to look.

### Freedom House Museum: 1315 Duke Street

Begin at the **Freedom House Museum**, housed in the building that served as the headquarters of Franklin & Armfield, one of the largest domestic slave-trading firms in the antebellum United States. Between 1828 and 1836, Franklin & Armfield shipped an estimated 1,000 enslaved people per year from Alexandria to the cotton fields of Louisiana and Mississippi — roughly the entire enslaved population of the city turned over every year.

The museum occupies the original building. Its exhibitions are thorough, honest, and powerful. Allow at least an hour. This is one of the most important African American heritage sites in the country and receives a fraction of the visitors it deserves.

### The Slave Trade Route

From Freedom House, walk north on Duke Street and then east on King Street, following in reverse the route that enslaved people were marched from the Franklin & Armfield compound to the wharves on the Potomac. Contemporary accounts describe coffles — groups of people chained together — being marched through these streets to waiting ships. The same streets you walk as a tourist were walked under compulsion by thousands of human beings.

At the waterfront, a marker near the foot of Duke Street acknowledges this history.

### Alfred Street Baptist Church: 301 South Alfred Street

Walk west on Duke Street to **Alfred Street Baptist Church**, one of the oldest African American congregations in the country, organized in the early 1800s. The current building dates to 1924 but the congregation's roots reach back to the city's founding era. Alfred Street Baptist has been central to Alexandria's African American community for over two centuries; during the civil rights movement it was a hub of organizing activity.

### Contraband and Freedmen's Cemetery

Walk north on Alfred Street and then east on Church Street to the **Contraband and Freedmen's Cemetery** at South Washington Street and Church Street. During the Civil War, thousands of enslaved people escaped to Union-held Alexandria, where they were classified as "contraband of war." When they died — from disease, exposure, and the consequences of decades of enslavement — they were buried here, in a cemetery that fell into complete disrepair after the war and was eventually paved over for a commercial strip.

Archaeological excavation beginning in 2007 confirmed the location and extent of the cemetery. The site has been restored as a memorial park, with a striking bronze sculpture by artist Mario Chiodo honoring the 1,800 or more people buried here.

Stand at this memorial and consider what is beneath your feet: the remains of people who came to Alexandria seeking freedom and found only its threshold. This is one of the most important public spaces in Northern Virginia.

## Chapter 7: Walk Five — Seminary Hill and the High Ground

**Distance:** 3.5 miles  
**Estimated Time:** 2.5 hours  
**Terrain:** Hilly; mix of sidewalk and unpaved path  
**Best Time:** Fall foliage (October–November) or spring bloom (April)

### Going Uphill

Most visitors to Alexandria never leave the flat riverfront grid. Seminary Hill, in the western part of the city, offers a completely different experience: genuine topography, woodland walks, sweeping views, and an atmosphere of quiet far removed from the King Street bustle.

### George Washington Masonic National Memorial

Begin at the **George Washington Masonic National Memorial**, perched on Shooter's Hill at the western end of King Street. This massive neoclassical tower, completed in 1932 and modeled loosely on the ancient Lighthouse of Alexandria in Egypt, rises 333 feet above the surrounding terrain and is visible for miles.

The Memorial houses an extraordinary collection of Washington artifacts, including the trowel he used to lay the cornerstone of the U.S. Capitol. Its observation tower offers the best aerial view of Alexandria available without a drone — on clear days you can see the Capitol dome, the Washington Monument, and the sweep of the Potomac Valley.

From the Memorial's plaza, pause and look east: you are looking at the entire old town spread below you, with the Potomac beyond and Maryland on the far shore. This is the view the city's founders had in mind.

### Virginia Theological Seminary

Walk south from the Masonic Memorial along Seminary Road to the **Virginia Theological Seminary**, founded in 1823 and one of the oldest Episcopal theological schools in the United States. The campus is open to the public and offers a remarkable architectural landscape: Gothic Revival stone buildings from the mid-19th century, mature trees that have been growing for 150 years, and a quiet that is startling so close to a major city.

The Seminary's **Immanuel Chapel**, completed in 1881, is an excellent example of Victorian Gothic ecclesiastical architecture. The library houses an important collection of early American religious materials.

Walk the campus perimeter, noting the earthworks visible in the southwestern corner — Civil War fortifications built by Union forces to protect this strategic high ground.

### Quaker Lane and the Woodland Path

From the Seminary, walk east on Quaker Lane, a road named for the Quaker meeting house that once stood in this area. At the intersection with Russell Road, pick up the unpaved path that winds through the wooded ravine known as **Chinquapin Park**.

This small natural area, bisected by a stream called Holmes Run, is one of the surprising wild spaces that persist within Alexandria's urban fabric. The woodland trail crosses the stream twice on wooden footbridges and passes through stands of tulip poplar, red maple, and ironwood. In spring the understory is carpeted with Virginia bluebells; in fall the canopy turns gold and orange.

The trail exits at the **Chinquapin Recreation Center** on King Street, from which you can walk east back to Old Town or catch a bus.

## Chapter 8: Walk Six — Arlandria / Chirilagua

**Distance:** 2 miles  
**Estimated Time:** 1.5 hours  
**Terrain:** Flat paved sidewalks  
**Best Time:** Weekend afternoon; lunchtime on any day

### The Other Alexandria

North of Old Town and Del Ray, where Alexandria's city limits press against Arlington County, lies Arlandria — a neighborhood known locally as Chirilagua after the Salvadoran coastal town from which many of its residents emigrated. This is working-class Alexandria, a world away from the antique shops and wine bars of King Street, and one of the most interesting places to walk in the entire city.

### Mount Vernon Avenue North

Begin at the northern end of Mount Vernon Avenue, above the Del Ray commercial strip. Here the avenue transitions from Del Ray's gentrified boutiques to a denser, more functional streetscape: Latin grocery stores, money transfer shops, a Salvadoran bakery whose smells stop you in your tracks, barbershops with hand-lettered signs, a Vietnamese noodle house operating from what appears to be a converted residential kitchen.

Walk north slowly. This is a neighborhood of small businesses that exist to serve their community, not to attract tourism, and there is a directness and efficiency about the streetscape that Old Town's curated prettiness can never quite achieve.

### Murals of Chirilagua

The neighborhood's community organizations have commissioned a series of murals along Mount Vernon Avenue and its side streets. These works — created by local artists, many of them Salvadoran or Central American — depict community history, traditional cultural imagery, portraits of neighborhood figures, and scenes of daily life. A self-guided mural walk map is available from the City of Alexandria's arts office.

The murals are not on the tourist maps. Finding them requires walking the side streets and paying attention to building walls. This is the correct approach.

### Four Mile Run

At the northern edge of Chirilagua, **Four Mile Run** descends from the west to empty into the Potomac. The stream has been channelized in concrete through much of its urban course, but the trail along its lower reach — which forms the boundary between Alexandria and Arlington — is a pleasant linear park.

Walk east along Four Mile Run toward the Potomac. The confluence of the stream and the river, at the foot of Commonwealth Avenue, offers views north toward the Key Bridge and the towers of Rosslyn, and south toward Old Town. It is a genuinely panoramic spot that almost no one visits.

## Chapter 9: Seasonal Walks and Special Occasions

### Cherry Blossoms (Late March–Early April)

Alexandria's waterfront plantings include a substantial number of ornamental cherry trees that bloom in late March or early April — slightly later than the famous Washington Mall plantings, which means you can see cherry blossoms in Alexandria even after the D.C. peak has passed. The best clusters are along the waterfront trail between Founders Park and Oronoco Bay Park.

### Fall Foliage (Mid-October–Early November)

Seminary Hill and the Chinquapin Park woodland trail are at their best in mid-to-late October, when the tulip poplars and maples turn. The neighborhood streets of Old Town, lined with mature street trees, are equally spectacular; the contrast of autumn foliage against Federal brick is one of Alexandria's signature visual experiences.

### Christmas Season (December)

Alexandria's December cityscape is worth a walk specifically for the decorations. The Historic District Association organizes a **Scottish Walk** parade on the first weekend of December — Highland bagpipe bands marching through Old Town, drawing enormous crowds. The city's Christmas decorations are elaborate, particularly along King Street and in the residential squares.

### Evening Walks

Old Town is beautiful after dark. The gas-style streetlamps (actually electric, but designed to evoke the gas era) along the historic streets cast a warm light over the brick and cobblestone. The waterfront promenade at night, with the lights of Maryland reflected in the Potomac, is one of the most romantic walks in the Washington region.

The Torpedo Factory Art Center holds evening events several times a month; check their calendar before you go.

## Chapter 10: Notes on Walking Well in a Historic City

### On Slowing Down

The fundamental discipline of walking a historic city is slowness. Speed is the enemy of observation. The walkers who remember Alexandria are those who stopped to read the historical markers, looked up at the architectural details above the shop windows, noticed the alley between buildings that opened into an unexpected courtyard.

Old Town alone has more than 100 buildings that predate 1800. Most of them have historical plaques mounted near their doors. Reading every plaque on a single walk of King Street would take an hour and transform your understanding of the street entirely. Try it.

### On Getting Lost

The best thing you can do in Alexandria is ignore this guide entirely for a while and simply walk in a direction that interests you. The grid makes getting lost impossible in any serious sense — you can always find the river to the east and the Metro tower to the west. Within that framework, the side streets of Old Town, Del Ray, and the other neighborhoods reward aimless exploration far more than systematic coverage.

Turn left when you were planning to go straight. Walk down the alley. Follow the sound of music. Alexandria is compact enough that every deviation eventually resolves.

### On Talking to People

Alexandrians are, on the whole, remarkably friendly to curious strangers. The owners of the small shops on King Street, the staff at the Torpedo Factory, the regulars at the Del Ray coffee shops — they know their city's history and are generally pleased when someone seems genuinely interested.

Ask the woman behind the counter at the apothecary about the bottle collection. Ask the docent at Gadsby's Tavern what the most surprising thing they have ever learned about the building. Ask the farmer at the Saturday market what they are growing this year. These conversations are part of the walk.

### On Documentation

Carry a small notebook — physical paper, not a phone. The act of writing by hand slows you down in a productive way, forces you to choose what you record, and produces a document that survives the death of software platforms. A walk journal from Alexandria will outlast any photo album.

## Chapter 11: Practical Reference

### Getting Around

**By Metro:** King Street–Old Town Station (Blue and Yellow Lines) is the primary entry point. Braddock Road Station serves the northern end of Old Town and Del Ray.

**By Bus:** DASH (Alexandria's local bus system) and Metrobus serve all neighborhoods described in this guide. The AT-1 (formerly the DASH 1) runs along King Street and Duke Street; the AT-2 runs along Mount Vernon Avenue through Del Ray and Arlandria.

**King Street Trolley:** Free, runs the length of King Street between the Metro and the waterfront, daily. Useful if your feet give out.

**By Bike:** Alexandria's bike infrastructure has improved substantially in recent years. The Potomac Appalachian Trail Club (PATC) maps are useful for off-road routes. The Four Mile Run Trail connects Alexandria to the larger regional trail network.

**By Water Taxi:** The Potomac Riverboat Company operates water taxis between the Alexandria waterfront and the National Mall. This is a delightful way to arrive or depart if you are combining Alexandria with a D.C. visit.

## Afterword: Why We Walk

There is a philosophical tradition, running from Rousseau through Wordsworth through Thoreau to Rebecca Solnit, that understands walking as thinking made physical — that the rhythm of steps unlocks something in the mind that sitting still cannot reach. That tradition is correct, as far as it goes. But walking a city is something more specific.

When you walk Alexandria, you are doing something that has been done on these same streets for nearly 300 years. The grid has changed at the edges; the surfaces have been repaired and replaced. But the distances are the same. The relationship between the market square and the river is unchanged. The air off the Potomac in the evening, the way the light hits the brick in the late afternoon — these are not reconstructions. They are the thing itself.

There is a continuity available to the walker that no vehicle can offer. You move at the pace the city was designed for. You encounter the city at the scale of a human body. You are, for the duration of your walk, contemporary with everyone who has ever walked these same blocks — the tobacco merchants, the enslaved people marched to the waterfront, the Union soldiers in their camps, the Victorian families in their Sunday best, the 20th-century residents who fought to save the waterfront warehouses, the neighbors walking their dogs this morning.

This book has tried to make that continuity visible: to show you not just where to go, but why it matters that you go there on foot, slowly, paying attention.

Alexandria rewards the walker who brings curiosity, time, and a willingness to be surprised. Go find out what I missed.
